Custom software used to need a business case. Months of build time meant you only wrote software for problems big enough to justify it. Everything smaller got a spreadsheet and a workaround.

That maths is gone. With AI doing the assembly, a working tool — a tracker, a booking flow, a report generator — ships in hours. Not a demo. Deployed, on a real URL, doing real work. I've shipped a second-market version of a working product in a day.

Most people haven't updated their instincts to match. Three that changed for me:

**Build for one client.** Software economics used to demand many customers to spread the cost. Now the cost is an afternoon, so software for *exactly one client's* workflow is viable — and it fits them better than any off-the-shelf product could. Charge for the outcome, not the code.

**Build to throw away.** When rebuilding is cheap, being wrong is cheap. Skip the long requirements document — build the rough version, put it in front of the user, and let the prototype be the spec. Version two costs less than the meeting to plan it.

**Say yes to small jobs.** The internal tool nobody would quote for, the one-off report format, the little automation between two systems — these were never worth custom software. Now they are. The backlog of "too small to build" is where the easy wins sit.

One caution: speed moved the bottleneck, it didn't remove it. Building is cheap; **knowing what's worth building, and verifying it actually works, is not.** A wrong tool shipped in two hours is still a wrong tool. The judgment — what to build, whether it's right, whether you'd stand behind the output — is still the job.

The scarce skill is no longer construction. It's selection and verification. Spend your time there.
